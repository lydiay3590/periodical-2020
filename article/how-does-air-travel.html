How Does Air Travel?

By Jianfeng Li

Air is the gaseous substance around us. It’s made of 78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen, and 1% other substances. Everyone talks about how oxygen is important because it helps transport blood throughout our body, but nitrogen is just as important because our body parts need nitrogen to stay healthy. The 1% of substances in the air are called aerosols, which are solid or liquid particles that are in the air. Moreover, air also contains water. This is because the air around us can lose heat, causing water to condense into its liquid form. This is described as humidity; we would say the air is more humid when there are more water particles in them.

Wind, a moving air, surrounds us every day. The direction that the wind is coming in, or the strength of the wind, depends on its pressure. According to LeChatelier’s principle, a set of principles that scientists use to predict change based on certain stress on a chemical system, air molecules will move from areas of high pressure to areas of low pressure because the system wants to maintain equilibrium. For example, let's say you are sitting on a bench, and you feel a wind coming at you from your right. This means that the air pressure on your right is greater than the air pressure on your left. The power of the wind depends on the difference in pressure between the 2 points. Comparing the wind moving around you outside of your house, to the wind that’s gushing out of a balloon, and we can easily see that the difference in pressure determines the strength of the wind.

The change in air pressure can be caused by temperature, altitude, and moisture. Since air molecules in cold air are more compacted, cold air becomes denser than hot air. This means that cold air is higher in pressure and therefore moves toward hotter areas. Altitude also affects pressure because higher altitudes have fewer molecules, meaning it has less pressure. Moisture makes the molecules in the air less dense because water has less mass than oxygen and nitrogen molecules, which lowers the pressure as well.

Different types of atoms also affect the direction of the air. Some balloons can float up because the atoms of the gas inside the balloon are lighter than the atoms of the air outside of the balloon. Since the density of the gas inside of the balloon is greater than the density of the air outside of the balloon, the balloon will float up. The opposite happens when the atoms inside of the balloon are denser than the atoms outside of the balloon, making the balloon fall instead of float.
